GalNav starlight + pulsar navigation demo How it works

Interstellar navigation, live

A photo of stars in, your position out.

Drop in a raw star-field image — from a telescope or a spacecraft, with no coordinates attached. The tool plate-solves it, finds the nearby Gaia stars in the frame, and intersects the lines of sight to recover where the camera is — in au, with an error ellipsoid. Same physics as the science spine; this is just a friendlier shell. No image handy? A reproducible New Horizons demo is below, and runs fully offline.

A detected blob is a spot of light; identifying it names the catalogued star and its distance; a position-capable star sits close enough that its parallax shift reveals where the spacecraft is. Most blobs are distant field stars beyond the nearby catalog — only the amber ones can navigate.

Walk through the pipeline step by step →

How it works & honest limits. Five stages: plate-solve → centroid → age the catalog → identify nearby stars → intersect the lines of position.
  • One image gives a line of position, not a point — you need ≥2 different nearby stars.
  • A random photo of the sky usually has no star within 20 pc in frame, so it can't be fixed (pointing still works).
  • An uploaded image with no embedded WCS needs a blind solver (local astrometry.net via WSL, or a nova API key).
  • The ground-based frames fix the observer — they land on Earth. The demo (New Horizons) frames land at ~47 au.

Sky-survey plates shown are from the STScI Digitized Sky Survey. The Digitized Sky Surveys were produced at the Space Telescope Science Institute under U.S. Government grant NAG W-2166. The images of these surveys are based on photographic data obtained using the Oschin Schmidt Telescope on Palomar Mountain and the UK Schmidt Telescope. 3-D view built on spacekit.js (Ian Webster, MIT), vendored offline.
