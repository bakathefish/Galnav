in

Rebuild each star's 3-D velocity from its public motion, then slide it forward along a straight line to the chosen age.

v = v_r*u + d*(mu_a*e_east + mu_d*e_north) r(t) = r0 + v * t

why the star map is a 2016 snapshot; stars drift; move them to "now" before matching.

04

Identify — "which dots are our stars?"

gui/locate.py :: identify_in_frame
join
in
WCS + centroids + aged positions + radiusmatch radius = 120 arcsec

Project every aged star through the WCS to a predicted pixel; keep those inside the frame; match each to the nearest centroid within the radius, one-to-one, closest wins.

why 120 arcsec swallows parallax — a star at distance d seen from range r shifts by up to r/d (36 arcsec for Proxima at 47 au). That shift is the signal stage 5 uses.

out
matches[ { star_index, centroid_index, sep_arcsec } ]
for each match: line of position { star_pos_au (3,), direction_unit (3,) }
05

Fix — "so where are we?"

gui/locate.py :: fix_position → galnav/nav/triangulate.py :: n_star_solve
reuses spine
in
lines of position≥ 2, from ≥ 2 different stars

Each star + measured direction is a line the craft must lie on. Weighted least squares finds the point closest to all the lines.

x = (sum_i w_i)^-1 (sum_i w_i p_i) w_i = (I - d_i d_i^T) / |p_i|^2

why distant stars are down-weighted by 1/|p|^2 (Lauer 2025's estimator, reused unchanged).

out
fix{ x_au: (3,), cov: (3x3), ellipsoid_au: (3,), chi2 }
POSITION: x (3,) au + 1-sigma error ellipsoid

The age loop — "what year is it?"

Same five stages, run once per candidate age. A wrong age propagates the stars to the wrong place, so their lines miss each other — high chi2. The best age is the bottom of the curve.

for each age

grid of candidate ages, e.g. 0 to 25 yr step 0.25

→
re-run 3–5

age the catalog, identify, fix

gui/age.py :: estimate_age

→
record

the fix's chi2 (how badly the lines miss a common point)

→
pick minimum

lowest-chi2 age; parabola curvature gives the +/- error bar

loops over the whole age grid — unmatchable ages score chi2 = infinity (guarded, never crashes)  =>  out: age_hat +/- sigma_yr

It works because nearby stars move fast: Proxima drifts 3.85 arcsec/yr, Wolf 359 4.7 arcsec/yr — a 4-year drift is several LORRI pixels, easily measured.

04

How to showcase it at the booth

Six moves in the browser, fully offline. The demo frames are in data/e3_new_horizons/repo/lor_*_pwcs2.fits; the full booth script (with the chronometer second finale) is in docs/ISEF-DEMO-PLAYBOOK.md.

1

Launch

Double-click Start GalNav Demo.bat (or run python -m gui.webapp) — the demo opens in your browser, no internet needed.

2

Full solve (all 12)

In the reproducible New Horizons card, click Full solve (all 12). The 12 lor_*_pwcs2.fits frames plate-solve from their headers instantly (or click Quick demo (2 frames) for the ~1 au teaching version).

3

Read the star labels

The preview labels every star in three tiers: cyan circles = every detected dot, distance labels = identified catalog stars, amber cross = the position-capable nearby star ("Proxima Cen (1.3 pc)"). The caption reads e.g. 100 detected - 2 identified - 1 position-capable.

say "Almost every dot here is too far to navigate by — the tool labels the one nearby star that actually carries our position."

4

Locate spacecraft

Twelve lines of position give miss ≈ 0.387 au (|r| ≈ 47.4 au), the 3-D position and the error ellipsoid — under 0.1 s.

say "A real spacecraft, found from its own photos of the stars — to a third of an au with all twelve frames."

5

See it in OpenSpace

Push the fix from the See it in OpenSpace panel: the amber sphere is the recovered fix, out past Pluto near the real New Horizons, with the cyan JPL-truth sphere 0.387 au away and the white miss line between. Every push is execution-confirmed by the engine's reply.

say "There we are, out past Pluto — and here is that same fix against the nearby stars it was found from."

6

Estimate catalog age — and the chronometer

Draws the chi2-vs-age curve; prints 4.286 +/- 0.055 yr against the true ~4.31 yr. For the second finale, upload a real 1953 DSS plate and it dates the photo to 1953.3 from one star's drift (negative catalog age — the star run backwards).

say "It reads what year it is from how far the stars have drifted — to about three weeks on the spacecraft frames, and it dated a seventy-year-old photograph to within a year."

06

What it does not do (say this before a judge asks)

A random sky photo gives pointing only

It contains none of the 1941 within-20-pc stars, so there is nothing near enough to shift with viewpoint. That is correct physics, not a bug — only nearby stars carry position.

One image = a line, not a point

A single star fixes you to a line of position. You need two images of two different nearby stars to pin a point. The tool says so plainly.

WCS-less images need a backend

A raw PNG with no plate solution needs a blind solver: a free nova API key, or a local WSL astrometry.net install. Every demo frame already carries its WCS, so the booth runs offline.

The ellipsoid is random noise only

The 1-sigma error bar counts centroid scatter, not un-modelled systematics. The 0.98 au miss still sits inside its own ellipsoid (0.9 sigma), so it is honest.
